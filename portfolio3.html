Geoscientific Simulations

My tenure at UT Austin, Los Alamos National Lab, and USC was marked by extensive work in geoscientific simulations. These projects ranged from modeling subsurface flow to simulating geological formations. Each project presented unique challenges, such as handling large datasets and creating accurate simulation models. My contributions not only advanced the computational aspects of these projects but also provided valuable insights into earth sciences, demonstrating the power of computational modeling in understanding and solving complex geoscientific problems.
My natural inclination post PhD was to pursue a postdoc, to experience a national lab environment, and I went about working with a team of excellent scientists at LANL on a graph based reduced order model to deliver fast yet accurate estimates of production to rig operators in hydrocarbon projects.
As I gradually started to realize that I shall be pursuing an industry career for a longer term, I published a paper, and moved on to an opportunity to work on an Army funded project at Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute in upstate NY. A funding issue notwithstanding, I did get an interesting opportunity to work on a consulting project with a startup in NYC during my time at RPI. Around that time, I also realized the impediment of the visa process, which disallowed me from joining a company although we had excellent agreement of mutual value we bring to the table. I started taking steps to address the fact that I would have to pursue my green card seriously, and I moved on to a bioinformatics gig at Baylor College of Medicine, with the sole purpose of publishing a paper with a ton of citations. A month after starting at BCM, the United States shutdown in response to the pandemic, and that hit me hard as I lost the opportunity to work with the colleagues at BCM on-site. A difficult period followed, and I was left with a simple choice of being very careful of the next position I choose, as this shall be the final one before I get my green card. Such opportunity presented itself in the form of a postdoc at University of Southern California, and I primary job right off the gate was to add to the code framework the same algorithm that had presented itself with so much success during my PhD.
