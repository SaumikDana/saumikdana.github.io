University of Texas at Austin

I started in Fall 2012 at the Department of Engineering Mechanics, on course to a classic Application Engineer working out at places like Abaqus, and Ansys as a career path starting step post PhD. The first problem I solved was a hydraulic fracturing code refactoring to stub out a cleaner way to understand how fractures branch out under hydraulic pressure inside the cracks. Along the way, I understood the impact of algorithms and how the underlying mathematics needs to be comprehended to genuinely make progress in terms of making things faster and accurate. In the Fall of 2014, I switched to an advisor at the renowned Institute for Computational Engineering, right across the road from the Department of Engineering Mechanics, and started tackling problems on the intersection of mechanics, high performance computing, and algorithms. The crux of the PhD thesis was the solution of this problem.

The idea being that the injection or production wells in the reservoir section of the domain causes geomechanical reaction which have implications on the free surface, albeit on a different length scale. Prior to the implementation of my algorithm, there was no way in-house for the code framework to pull off a simulation of such magnitude. With some sustained work in high performance computing, iterative solution techniques, and coding in a mixed programming language environment, I was able to generate simulations of free surface displacements along side pressure evolution almost 10000 feet beneath the free surface, at the same time. The first step was to do the hard yards of understanding the mixed language framework with the MPI implementation, and then the iterative sequential solution technique. I arrived at a method that allows leveraging the strengths of the code and the technique, and I went about the task over a 2 year period lending to the images above. Right after I published my first paper, which was highly succesful, I went about a mathematical analysis to satisfy the wishes of my advisor, who is an Applied Mathematician. The big takeway from my PhD was the ability to take apart complicated code frameworks, and dissect a solution technique that is minimally invasive, and yet a novelty. Ever since, this has served me well.

Los Alamos National Lab

My natural inclination post PhD was to pursue a postdoc, to experience a national lab environment, and I went about working with a team of excellent scientists at LANL on a graph based reduced order model to deliver fast yet accurate estimates of production to rig operators in hydrocarbon projects.
